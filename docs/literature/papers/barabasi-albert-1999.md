# Barabasi & Albert (1999)

**Emergence of Scaling in Random Networks.** *Science*, 286(5439), 509-512.

- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.286.5439.509
- Free (arXiv cond-mat/9910332): https://arxiv.org/abs/cond-mat/9910332

## What it shows (in our words)

Many real networks have degree distributions that follow a power law: a few
nodes (hubs) have very many connections while most have few. The authors explain
this with **preferential attachment** - new nodes connect preferentially to
already-popular nodes, so "the rich get richer." Growth plus preferential
attachment generates the heavy tail.

## What we borrow

Preferential attachment as the generative reason that access (connections,
opportunities) is power-law distributed rather than uniform.

## How it maps to the model

It justifies the heavy-tailed, environment-correlated **opportunity** process in
section 7.5: arrival rates scale with where you are and who you know, so access
concentrates. It pairs with Merton's Matthew effect (cumulative advantage) and
with the Pareto payoff distribution to make outcomes power-law.
