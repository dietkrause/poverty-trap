# Merton (1968)

**The Matthew Effect in Science.** *Science*, 159(3810), 56-63.

- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1126/science.159.3810.56
- Open scans are widely available.

## What it shows (in our words)

Recognition and reward accrue disproportionately to those who already have a
reputation - eminent scientists get outsized credit for similar work, while
lesser-known contributors get less. Merton names this cumulative advantage the
"Matthew effect" (to those who have, more is given).

## What we borrow

Cumulative advantage: an early lead compounds into a larger one, independent of
underlying merit.

## How it maps to the model

It is the social-science statement of the multiplicative dynamics we use:
returns on existing capital (section 7.4) and connection-driven opportunity
access (7.5-7.6) both give more to those who already have. Together with the
Kesten/power-law mechanics (Pluchino note), it explains why advantage
concentrates even when underlying talent does not.
