# Chetty, Hendren, Kline & Saez (2014)

**Where is the Land of Opportunity? The Geography of Intergenerational Mobility
in the United States.** *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 129(4), 1553-1623.

- DOI: https://doi.org/10.1093/qje/qju022
- Free (NBER working paper w19843): https://www.nber.org/papers/w19843

## What it shows (in our words)

Using de-identified tax records for millions of US families, the authors measure
how strongly a child's adult income depends on where they grew up. Upward
mobility varies enormously across regions; some places reliably lift low-income
children and others do not. Mobility is, to a large degree, *geographic*: the
neighbourhood you are born into shapes your trajectory.

## What we borrow

The result that the starting environment sets the baseline rate of progress, not
just the starting level.

## How it maps to the model

It justifies the **zone** and the per-zone **base drift** `mu_base(z)` in section
7.4: a poor neighbourhood carries a negative base drift, a rich one a positive
drift. It also motivates the social-network channel (the 2022 social-capital
follow-up), since part of "place" is who you are connected to.
